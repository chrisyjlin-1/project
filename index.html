Northwestern students feel impact of recent restaurant closures
By Janelle, Natalie, Christina, Sylvie and Elias
When students need to get away from campus, they don’t have to travel far. Downtown Evanston is home to several restaurants, coffee shops, grocery stores, clothing shops and other businesses that give students an outlet from their busy class schedules during the week and on weekends.

But several popular restaurants and cafes have shuttered since May 2025, leaving Northwestern students with fewer spots to socialize, enjoy a non-dining hall meal or even go on dates.

Here are just some of those closures we mapped out:

“I felt like I was robbed,” Medill junior Alexander Hernandez Gonzalez recalls feeling after receiving a swarm of texts from friends telling him about Chili’s’ closure last December.

Hernandez Gonzalez went a few times each quarter, though he visited more often during the spring.

He had his order set: the margarita of the month, chips and salsa and Chili’s’ famous Triple Dipper. His signature combo included Nashville hot mozzarella sticks, hot chicken crispers and, for the third option, either burger bites or Southwest egg rolls. If he wasn’t too full, he would add a lava cake for dessert.

But Chili’s wasn’t just a place for Hernandez Gonzalez to satisfy his cravings. It was also a place where he grew closer with friends.

“The food, atmosphere and conversations that were had there was truly something that you can’t replicate in other spots in Evanston,” Hernandez Gonzalez said.

As upset as he is about the closure, so much so that he wrote a memoriam for it in The Daily Northwestern, he said the community ultimately has to move on. But others weren’t ready to let it go just yet.

Fourth-year Northwestern student Owen Polley created a Change.org petition to save Bob’s Pizza. In the description, he wrote, “Unreal. This city is taking away everything we love, everything we hold dear to heart. We can’t stand for it. We must fight back. Save Bob's Pizza.” The petition received 200 signatures.

Polley said that Bob’s had been a place for him and his friends to hang out since freshman year. He shared that while the petition ultimately did not achieve the outcome he was hoping for, he is proud that it “showed a lot of the love for Bob’s in the community and how much it meant to students.”

Similar petitions were created for Chili’s, which received 612 signatures, and Peet’s Coffee, which received 685. Students also reported signing petitions for Happy Lemon, Coffee Lab and Reza’s Restaurant, although as of March 2026, we were unable to locate these petitions online.

With a rigorous school like Northwestern, students often spend much of their time studying in the library, but many switch it up by studying or meeting friends at a coffee shop. But with one less coffee spot on campus, some students are searching for a new place to settle in

For Medill senior Lily Ogburn, going to Peet’s Coffee was an integral part of her weekly routine. At least three days a week, Ogburn would grab a spot — usually the window table at Peet’s, which she noted was especially nice for the natural light from the large windows — and grind out work on her senior thesis. She was fueled by her go-to order: an iced vanilla latte and a chocolate croissant.

As soon as she heard about the closure, she said she was devastated. It's been over a month since it closed, and Ogburn still hasn’t found a new coffee spot to call home.

“I’ve been bouncing around to a few different ones, but I haven’t found one that I’m obsessed with or go to every time,” Ogburn said.

But other Northwestern students might be able to help with that. In the Evanston closure survey, students were asked where they are going instead of Peet’s, with Colectivo and Starbucks taking the lead, along with a third-place response of students frequenting on-campus coffee shops more often. Even if Peet’s isn’t coming back, students have begun finding alternatives.

During the making of this project, the editors realized how fragile neighborhood spaces and the memories they hold can be. Once a restaurant closes, every laughter, every coversation it has carried disappears with it. The tables where students lingered over coffee, the corners where friends met week after week, the small routines that quietly shaped daily life, these collective memories close doors to us as well as these spaces shut down. Unlike buildings or landmarks, these everyday interiors rarely leave behind a trace.

If these spaces, Chili's, Peet's, Bob's, vanish so easily, perhaps the other beloved places should be recorded while they are still alive. The cafés featured in this section, selected by our survey participants, remain open today, continuing to host the ordinary rituals of the neighborhood. Capturing them now turns the VR not only into a tour, but into a spatial archive, a small act of documenting Evanston’s living social geography.

In these virtual spaces, we bottled a moment of this town and our community. As routines migrate and neighborhoods adapt, the spaces that remain carry the quiet afterlife of what came before.

Ten years from now, Evanston will likely look different again. Some of these cafés may still be here; others may have quietly disappeared and been replaced by new interiors and new routines. What remains, however, is the record, the moments of archive within the shifting landscape of urban life.
